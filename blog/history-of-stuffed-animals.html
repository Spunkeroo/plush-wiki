The History of Stuffed Animals: From Teddy Bears to NFT Plushies

Published February 23, 2026 • By the Plush.Wiki Encyclopedia Team • 18 min read

The stuffed animal is one of the most universal objects in human experience. Across cultures, continents, and centuries, people have created soft, huggable representations of animals for comfort, companionship, play, and emotional expression. What began as hand-sewn cloth animals in the 19th century has evolved into a global industry worth over $14 billion, encompassing everything from mass-produced plush toys to artisanal creations, from collectible limited editions to digital-physical hybrid objects that blur the line between the tangible and the virtual.

This is the story of the stuffed animal — how it came to exist, how it evolved through the most transformative centuries in human history, and where it is headed next. It is a story about manufacturing and marketing, certainly, but it is also a story about human psychology, cultural values, and the enduring need for something soft to hold onto.

Before the Teddy Bear: Early Stuffed Animals (Pre-1900)

The concept of a soft toy made to resemble an animal predates the modern stuffed animal by centuries, possibly millennia. Archaeological evidence suggests that ancient Egyptian, Roman, and Chinese children played with simple cloth dolls and animal figures stuffed with rags, straw, or other available materials. These were folk objects, handmade within households, and none survive in large numbers.

The commercialization of stuffed animals began in Germany in the 1880s, and the name most closely associated with this origin is Margarete Steiff. Born in 1847 in Giengen an der Brenz, Germany, Steiff was confined to a wheelchair due to childhood polio but became a skilled seamstress. In 1880, she started a felt clothing business, and around 1880 she began making small felt elephant pincushions based on a pattern she found in a magazine. These elephant pincushions proved popular as children's toys rather than as sewing accessories, and Steiff recognized the commercial opportunity.

The Founding of Steiff

By 1893, Steiff had established a formal toy-making business and was exhibiting at the Leipzig Toy Fair, where her felt animals attracted significant attention. Her nephew, Richard Steiff, joined the company and brought a critical innovation: he had been sketching bears at the Stuttgart Zoo and proposed creating a bear toy with movable limbs. This jointed bear, made with mohair plush rather than felt, was first displayed at the Leipzig Toy Fair in 1903. According to company lore, it initially attracted little interest until an American buyer named Hermann Berg purchased 3,000 units for the American market.

The Steiff company introduced its famous "Button in Ear" trademark in 1904 — a small metal button attached to the left ear of each authentic Steiff animal. This was one of the first brand identification marks in the toy industry and remains the most recognized quality mark in plush manufacturing to this day.

The Birth of the Teddy Bear (1902-1903)

The teddy bear's origin story is one of the most repeated tales in toy history, and while some details have been embellished over retellings, the core narrative is well-documented.

In November 1902, President Theodore Roosevelt went on a bear-hunting trip in Mississippi. After several days without success, his attendants tracked, cornered, and tied a black bear to a tree, inviting Roosevelt to shoot it for his trophy. Roosevelt refused, considering it unsportsmanlike. The incident was captured in a political cartoon by Clifford Berryman in The Washington Post on November 16, 1902, depicting Roosevelt turning away from a small, pathetic-looking bear.

The cartoon inspired Morris Michtom, a Russian immigrant who ran a small toy store in Brooklyn, New York. Michtom and his wife Rose created a stuffed bear toy, displayed it in their shop window with a sign reading "Teddy's Bear" (a reference to Roosevelt's nickname "Teddy"), and the response was immediate and overwhelming. According to Michtom family accounts, he wrote to President Roosevelt asking permission to use his name, and Roosevelt reportedly replied that he doubted his name would be of much use to the stuffed bear business, but Michtom was welcome to use it. Michtom founded the Ideal Novelty and Toy Company, and the teddy bear became a national sensation.

"The teddy bear succeeded because it arrived at a moment when American culture was ready for it. The industrial revolution had created a middle class with disposable income and a desire for objects that represented warmth and domesticity. The teddy bear was both a product of industrialization and a reaction against it." — Rimel Gibreel, cultural historian

Simultaneous Invention

It is worth noting that the Steiff bear and the Michtom teddy bear were developed almost simultaneously but independently. Richard Steiff's jointed mohair bear was being developed in Germany at the same time Michtom was creating his stuffed bear in Brooklyn. Neither was aware of the other's work. This simultaneous invention suggests that the cultural moment was ripe for the stuffed bear on both sides of the Atlantic, driven by similar forces: industrialization, rising middle-class prosperity, changing attitudes toward childhood, and evolving relationships with the natural world.

The Golden Age of Stuffed Animals (1903-1950s)

The teddy bear craze that followed Michtom and Steiff's creations was extraordinary in its speed and scale. By 1906, teddy bears were being produced by dozens of manufacturers in the United States, Germany, and Britain. The bear had become a cultural phenomenon, inspiring songs ("The Teddy Bears' Picnic," composed in 1907), books, and a collecting mania that foreshadowed later crazes like Beanie Babies and Squishmallows.

Key Manufacturers of the Early 20th Century

Steiff (Germany, 1880-present) — Continued to set the standard for quality and innovation. Introduced new animals beyond bears, developed new materials, and maintained premium positioning. Steiff bears from this era are among the most valuable collectibles in the plush world, with pre-war examples regularly selling for thousands of dollars at auction.
Ideal Toy Company (USA, 1903-1997) — Morris Michtom's company grew from a Brooklyn toy shop to one of the largest toy manufacturers in America. Beyond teddy bears, Ideal produced a wide range of toys and dolls.
Merrythought (England, 1930-present) — Founded in Ironbridge, Shropshire, Merrythought brought British craftsmanship to stuffed animal production. Their bears and animals are characterized by quality mohair, careful hand-finishing, and distinctly British character. Still in production today and highly collectible.
Schuco (Germany, 1912-1976) — Known for innovative miniature bears and mechanical novelties. Schuco bears featured hidden compacts, perfume bottles, and even lighters inside their bodies. These clever designs make them prized by collectors.

Materials and Construction

Early stuffed animals were made primarily from mohair plush (woven from the hair of Angora goats), with excelsior (wood shavings) or kapok (a natural plant fiber) as filling. Joints were created using cardboard discs and metal pins, allowing limbs and heads to move. Glass or shoe-button eyes were standard, and noses were hand-embroidered. These materials gave early stuffed animals a distinctive look and feel that modern synthetic plush does not replicate, which is part of their enduring collectible appeal.

The post-World War II period saw the introduction of synthetic fabrics and polyester fiberfill, which dramatically reduced production costs and made stuffed animals accessible to a much wider market. This democratization of plush came at the expense of some of the artisanal quality that characterized the pre-war era, but it brought soft toys to children around the world who could never have afforded a mohair Steiff.

The Television Era and Character Plush (1950s-1980s)

The rise of television transformed the stuffed animal industry by creating a new category: character plush. Before television, stuffed animals were primarily generic representations of real animals (bears, dogs, cats, rabbits). Television created fictional characters that children formed emotional bonds with, and the stuffed animal became a vehicle for extending those bonds into the physical world.

Key Character Plush Milestones

Winnie the Pooh (1960s onward) — A.A. Milne's characters had been popular since the 1920s books, but Walt Disney's 1966 animated adaptation launched Pooh into the plush stratosphere. Winnie the Pooh plush has been in continuous production for over 60 years and remains one of the best-selling character plush lines in history.
Sesame Street (1969 onward) — Big Bird, Elmo, Cookie Monster, and the rest of the Sesame Street cast became some of the most recognizable stuffed characters of the 20th century. The "Tickle Me Elmo" craze of 1996 would later become a case study in toy marketing hysteria.
Care Bears (1983) — One of the first toy lines created specifically for merchandise potential (the characters were designed by American Greetings before the animated series existed). Care Bears demonstrated the power of designing characters with plush production in mind from the start.
Teddy Ruxpin (1985) — The first commercially successful animatronic stuffed animal. Teddy Ruxpin used cassette tapes to tell stories while moving its mouth and eyes. While primitive by today's standards, it represented the first major integration of technology into the stuffed animal format.

The Rise of Japanese Character Plush

Japan's contribution to character plush cannot be overstated. The founding of Sanrio in 1960 and the creation of Hello Kitty in 1974 established a model for character-driven merchandise that would influence the entire global toy industry. Hello Kitty demonstrated that a character did not need a television show, a movie, or even a narrative to become a merchandise juggernaut. The character's deliberately simple, expressionless design allowed consumers to project their own emotions onto it, creating a personal connection that drove decades of loyalty.

The launch of Pokemon in 1996 created what is arguably the largest single character plush franchise in history. The Pokemon Company's approach — creating hundreds of distinct creatures, each with a unique design, personality, and fanbase — provided an almost infinite canvas for plush production. As of 2026, the official Pokemon plush catalog exceeds thousands of unique designs, with the "Pokemon Fit" line alone aiming to produce a plush of every single Pokemon ever created.

The Beanie Baby Phenomenon (1993-1999)

No history of stuffed animals is complete without a detailed examination of the Beanie Baby craze, which remains the most extreme collecting mania in plush history and one of the most remarkable episodes in the broader history of collectibles.

Ty Warner, the founder of Ty Inc., launched Beanie Babies in 1993 with a simple but revolutionary concept: small, understuffed plush animals filled with plastic pellets ("beans") rather than traditional stuffing. The understuffing gave them a floppy, poseable quality that felt different from rigid, overstuffed competitors. They were priced at $5, making them impulse-buy affordable, and each one came with a heart-shaped hang tag containing a name, birthday, and short poem.

The Mechanics of Mania

Several factors combined to transform Beanie Babies from a popular toy into a full-blown speculative bubble:

  • Artificial scarcity: Ty Warner deliberately limited distribution, selling only through small gift shops and specialty retailers (not mass-market chains like Toys "R" Us). He also "retired" designs regularly, instantly creating scarcity and driving collectors to buy current designs before they disappeared.
  • The internet: The Beanie Baby craze coincided with the early days of consumer internet adoption. Online communities, price guides, and trading forums amplified the mania by connecting collectors nationwide and creating real-time price discovery. eBay, which launched in 1995, found that Beanie Babies were its most traded category in its early years.
  • Speculative frenzy: As prices for retired Beanie Babies escalated on the secondary market (with some rare examples selling for hundreds or even thousands of dollars), people began buying Beanie Babies not for enjoyment but as investments. This speculative demand further inflated prices and attracted more speculators in a classic bubble dynamic.
  • Media coverage: Newspapers, magazines, and television news programs covered the craze extensively, which brought in new participants and amplified the sense that Beanie Babies were a legitimate store of value.

The Crash

The bubble peaked around 1998-1999 and collapsed rapidly. Ty overproduced in response to demand, diluting scarcity. The speculative narrative fell apart as it became clear that most Beanie Babies would not appreciate in value. Many people who had invested thousands of dollars in Beanie Baby "portfolios" were left with collections worth a fraction of what they paid. The crash became a cautionary tale about speculative bubbles in collectibles markets.

However, the Beanie Baby craze left lasting legacies for the plush industry. It proved that adults would buy stuffed animals in massive quantities if given the right incentive structure. It demonstrated the power of artificial scarcity and retirement cycles. And it showed that plush toys could function as collectibles with secondary market value. Every subsequent plush collecting phenomenon — from Squishmallows to Jellycat — operates in the shadow of lessons learned (and mistakes made) during the Beanie Baby era.

The 2000s: Digital Integration and Build-A-Bear

The 2000s brought two significant developments to the stuffed animal world: the integration of digital experiences with physical plush, and the rise of experiential retail through Build-A-Bear Workshop.

Webkinz (2005)

Ganz's Webkinz line was a breakthrough concept: each stuffed animal came with a code that unlocked a virtual version of the pet on the Webkinz World website. Children could play games, decorate virtual rooms, and care for their digital pet. The combination of physical plush and digital engagement was enormously successful, with Webkinz becoming one of the best-selling toy lines of 2007. While the initial craze faded, the model of combining physical toys with digital experiences was influential and foreshadowed approaches that would be adopted by many toy companies in subsequent years.

Build-A-Bear Workshop (1997-present)

Founded by Maxine Clark in 1997, Build-A-Bear Workshop transformed the act of buying a stuffed animal into an interactive retail experience. Customers choose an unstuffed animal skin, help fill it at a stuffing station, add a heart (with a wishing ritual), and then dress and name their creation. The model was brilliantly designed to create emotional attachment through participation, and it generated significantly higher average transaction values than traditional retail plush.

Build-A-Bear has evolved significantly over the decades. Licensed character bears (Pokemon, Star Wars, Marvel, anime) now drive much of the business, and the "After Dark" adult-oriented line launched in the 2020s acknowledged and embraced the growing adult collector market. By 2026, Build-A-Bear operates hundreds of locations worldwide and has become a cultural institution that spans multiple generations of stuffed animal lovers.

The Squishmallow Revolution (2017-Present)

If the Beanie Baby craze was the stuffed animal industry's speculative bubble, the Squishmallow phenomenon is its sustained cultural shift. Launched by Kellytoy (now a Jazwares subsidiary) in 2017, Squishmallows reimagined the basic parameters of what a stuffed animal could be.

What Made Squishmallows Different

The core innovation was textural. Squishmallows are made from a proprietary polyester spandex fabric that feels like nothing else on the market — somewhere between a marshmallow and a stress ball. The round, stackable shape was practical (they double as pillows) and visually distinctive. The understated facial embroidery gave each character a gentle, approachable expression. And the character system — each Squishmallow has a name, personality, and backstory — created emotional hooks that encouraged collecting and community.

What truly distinguished Squishmallows from previous plush trends was the way collecting culture grew organically around the product. Social media, particularly TikTok and Instagram, became the engines of Squishmallow culture. Collectors shared "shelf tours," "hunting" videos (documenting trips to retail stores in search of specific Squishmallows), and trading content. The community-driven nature of Squishmallow collecting felt authentic in a way that corporate-engineered scarcity did not.

Squishmallows by the Numbers

By 2026, over 500 million Squishmallows have been sold worldwide. The line includes more than 2,000 unique characters across sizes from 3.5 inches to 24 inches. Squishmallows have been the number-one-selling plush line in North America for multiple consecutive years, and they show no signs of slowing. The brand has expanded into apparel, accessories, and a card game, but the core plush line remains the foundation of the phenomenon.

The Rise of Jellycat and Luxury Plush

While Squishmallows dominated the mass market, the 2020s also saw the rise of luxury plush as a recognized category. Jellycat, the London-based brand founded in 1999, emerged as the leading name in this space.

Jellycat's approach is the opposite of Squishmallows in almost every way. Where Squishmallows are round, uniform, and designed for mass production, Jellycat designs are idiosyncratic, textured, and visually diverse. Where Squishmallows retail at $5-$25, Jellycat plush ranges from $15 to $90+. Where Squishmallows thrives on variety and hunting, Jellycat creates demand through quality and the regular retirement of designs.

The result is a brand that has become a status symbol in certain circles — the plush equivalent of a Le Creuset pot or a Muji pen. Jellycat plush appears in carefully curated Instagram photos, on the desks of creative professionals, and in the nurseries of design-conscious parents. The brand demonstrated that there was a substantial market for premium plush that adults would buy for themselves, not just for children.

Digital Plush and the NFT Experiment (2021-2024)

The intersection of stuffed animals and digital technology took a new turn in the early 2020s with the rise of NFTs (non-fungible tokens) and blockchain-based collectibles. Several companies attempted to create "digital plush" — NFT collectibles that existed as digital images or 3D models on the blockchain, sometimes paired with physical plush counterparts.

What Happened

The NFT plush experiment yielded mixed results. A few projects successfully created communities around digital-physical hybrid collectibles. Pudgy Penguins, originally an NFT collection, launched a physical plush toy line in 2023 that became one of the surprise hits of the holiday season, proving that a digital-first brand could successfully translate to physical plush. The physical Pudgy Penguins plush included QR codes linking to digital experiences, creating a bridge between the blockchain community and the traditional toy market.

However, most NFT plush projects failed to gain traction, for the same reasons most NFT projects failed: the value proposition was unclear to mainstream consumers, the technology was intimidating, and the speculative element that drove initial interest evaporated when the broader NFT market cooled in 2023-2024. The lesson the industry took from the NFT era was not that digital integration is unworkable, but that the physical plush must stand on its own merits, with any digital component as an optional enhancement rather than the core value proposition.

What Survived

The useful concepts from the digital plush era have been adapted into more practical forms. NFC chips embedded in plush that link to authentication certificates, digital "birth certificates," or augmented reality experiences have proven viable. Some brands now include QR codes on tags that unlock content, verify authenticity, and connect to online communities. These approaches work because they enhance the physical plush experience without requiring the owner to understand or care about blockchain technology.

The Modern Plush Landscape (2026)

As of 2026, the plush toy industry is the most dynamic, diverse, and culturally significant it has ever been. Several defining characteristics distinguish the current moment.

Adults as a Primary Market

The most consequential shift of the past decade is the normalization of adult plush ownership and collecting. Market research from the NPD Group and Circana indicates that adults (18+) now account for over 40% of plush purchases, up from approximately 25% in 2019. This is not a passing trend but a generational shift. Millennials and Gen Z have brought plush into their homes, offices, and social identities in ways that previous generations did not. The stigma that once surrounded adult attachment to stuffed animals has largely evaporated in Western markets (it was never present in Japan, where adult engagement with kawaii culture has been mainstream for decades).

Sustainability and Ethical Production

Environmental consciousness has moved from a marketing angle to a baseline expectation. Major brands including Aurora ("Eco Nation"), Wild Republic ("EcoKins"), and even Squishmallows (select sustainable lines) now offer plush made from recycled materials, primarily PET plastic from post-consumer bottles. The quality of recycled-material plush has improved to the point where it is indistinguishable from conventional production, removing the last consumer resistance to sustainable options.

The Creator Economy and Independent Plush

Platforms like Makeship have democratized plush production, allowing independent creators, streamers, artists, and content creators to produce limited-run plush toys based on their own designs. This has created a vibrant independent plush scene that operates alongside the major brands, offering creativity and scarcity that mass-market plush cannot match. Some of the most innovative plush designs of 2026 come from independent creators rather than established toy companies.

Online Claw Machines

The digitization of the arcade experience through online claw machine platforms has created a new channel for plush acquisition that combines gaming, collecting, and the thrill of the win. Platforms like Claw.Pizza allow users to control real claw machines remotely, competing for genuine plush prizes that ship to their doors. This model extends the reach of claw machine culture far beyond the physical arcade and has introduced millions of people to the particular joy of winning rather than buying a stuffed animal.

The Psychology of Stuffed Animals

The enduring appeal of stuffed animals across cultures and centuries points to something deeper than marketing or nostalgia. Psychological research offers several explanations for why humans are drawn to soft, animal-shaped objects.

Transitional objects: Psychoanalyst Donald Winnicott coined the term "transitional object" in the 1950s to describe items (typically stuffed animals or blankets) that help children manage the emotional transition from dependence to independence. The stuffed animal represents a bridge between the comfort of the caregiver and the outside world. What is now understood is that adults also use transitional objects, and there is no developmental deadline after which this becomes inappropriate.

Touch and comfort: Soft textures activate the body's parasympathetic nervous system, reducing cortisol levels and promoting feelings of safety and calm. Holding a stuffed animal provides tactile stimulation that is physiologically calming, which is why weighted plush for anxiety relief has become such a significant market segment.

Anthropomorphism and companionship: Humans are hardwired to assign emotions and personalities to objects with face-like features. A stuffed animal with eyes and a mouth is perceived, at some level of cognitive processing, as a social being. This is not a sign of immaturity or delusion. It is a fundamental feature of human cognition that allows stuffed animals to provide a genuine sense of companionship.

Collecting and identity: A plush collection is a form of self-expression. The characters, brands, and aesthetics a person gravitates toward say something about who they are, what they value, and how they want to present themselves to the world. In an era where identity is increasingly curated across digital and physical spaces, a plush collection is both a personal comfort and a public statement.

Looking Ahead: The Future of Stuffed Animals

The stuffed animal has survived and thrived through industrialization, two world wars, the television era, the digital revolution, and the social media age. There is no reason to believe it will not continue to adapt and flourish. Several trajectories seem likely.

Material innovation: Advances in sustainable materials, including biodegradable synthetic fibers and lab-grown natural fibers, will continue to reshape plush manufacturing. The plush of 2030 may be made from materials that do not exist yet, but it will still need to be soft, huggable, and emotionally resonant.

Technology integration: Smart plush with embedded AI for conversational interaction, emotional sensing, and adaptive behavior will improve. The key constraint is that the technology must enhance, not replace, the fundamental plush experience. The most successful tech plush will be the ones that are perfect stuffed animals first and smart devices second.

Cultural expansion: As plush culture continues to globalize, new aesthetic traditions and character design philosophies from Africa, Latin America, South Asia, and other regions will enrich the global plush landscape. The current dominance of Western and Japanese design traditions will give way to a more diverse range of styles and sensibilities.

Digital-physical convergence: The relationship between physical plush and digital identities will continue to evolve. Online claw machine platforms like Claw.Pizza represent one form of this convergence, where digital interaction leads to physical ownership. Augmented reality experiences triggered by physical plush, blockchain-verified authentication, and community platforms that bridge online and offline collecting will all mature.

But through all of these changes, the essential appeal of the stuffed animal will remain what it has been since Margarete Steiff sewed her first felt elephant: something soft, something comforting, something that makes the world feel a little less harsh and a little more kind. That is not a need that technology can obsolete. It is a need that is woven into the human condition itself.

"The history of the stuffed animal is, in many ways, the history of human kindness made tangible. Every teddy bear, every plush rabbit, every Squishmallow is an object that exists because someone wanted to create something soft in a hard world." — Plush.Wiki
